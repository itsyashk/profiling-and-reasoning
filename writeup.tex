\section{Problem 2.4 -- Nsight Profiling}

\subsection*{Part 1}

For the small model at context length 128, the measured forward-pass NVTX range took 26.3 ms in Nsight Systems, which is in the same order as the Python timing but lower because this profile measured one steady-state step after warmup rather than averaging a longer run. The forward pass was dominated by the cuBLAS GEMM kernel \texttt{ampere\_sgemm\_128x64\_tn}, which took 81.818 ms cumulatively across the profile and was invoked 342 times total, or about 57 times per forward pass over the 6 profiled forward passes.

Besides matrix multiplication, nontrivial CUDA runtime came from elementwise multiply/add/copy kernels, sigmoid, attention masking via \texttt{where}, and softmax-related max, exp, divide, and sum-reduction kernels. When profiling both forward and backward, the same \texttt{ampere\_sgemm\_128x64\_tn} kernel remained the largest single cumulative kernel at 76.000 ms, but backward also introduced large GEMM kernels such as \texttt{ampere\_sgemm\_128x64\_nn} at 63.176 ms and \texttt{ampere\_sgemm\_128x64\_nt} at 58.041 ms, along with more elementwise and reduction work.

For a full AdamW training step, the measured NVTX range was 134.6 ms, with 44.9 ms in the measured forward pass, 50.3 ms in backward, and 46.6 ms in the optimizer step. Compared with inference, the fraction of time spent outside matrix multiplication increased because backward and AdamW add many elementwise update, copy, memset, and reduction kernels in addition to the GEMMs.

Within self-attention, the annotated ranges showed that softmax took 22.593 ms total, attention-score matmul took 12.558 ms, and the final attention matmul took 5.865 ms across the profile. Thus softmax took more runtime than the final attention matmul despite having far fewer FLOPs, which suggests it is more memory-bandwidth and kernel-launch limited than the highly optimized cuBLAS matrix multiplications.

\section{Problem 2.6 -- Memory Profiling}

\subsection*{Part 1}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{artifacts/memory/memory_large_ctx128_bs4_forward_fp32_pid5504.png}
\caption{Active memory timeline for a forward-only pass of the large model at context length 128.}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{artifacts/memory/memory_large_ctx128_bs4_train-step_fp32_pid25756.png}
\caption{Active memory timeline for a full training step of the large model at context length 128.}
\end{figure}

I added a \texttt{--use-memory-profiler} option to the benchmark script that starts
\texttt{torch.cuda.memory.\_record\_memory\_history} after warmup, profiles only the
measured step, dumps a snapshot under \texttt{artifacts/memory/}, and prints the
peak allocated and reserved CUDA memory. In the forward-only timeline, active
memory stays nearly flat around the model-weight baseline, with only small
transient allocations for activations and logits during the forward pass. In the
full training-step timeline, memory first ramps up during the forward pass as
activations are saved, drops through backward as those saved tensors are freed,
and then rises again during the optimizer step as gradients and Adam optimizer
state are materialized, producing a much larger peak around $6.5$ GiB.

\subsection*{Part 2}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{ccc}
\toprule
Context Length & Forward Peak Memory & Training-Step Peak Memory \\
\midrule
128 & 1656.4 MiB & 6701.1 MiB \\
256 & 1708.5 MiB & 6721.1 MiB \\
512 & 1920.8 MiB & 10814.3 MiB \\
1024 & 2729.9 MiB & OOM \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The forward-pass peak grows gradually with context length because the large
model's FP32 parameters are the dominant fixed cost, while activations and logits
scale with sequence length. The training-step peak is much larger because it
includes saved activations, gradients, and Adam optimizer state in addition to
the parameters; on my GPU, the context-length 1024 training step ran out of
memory while materializing the attention scores.

\subsection*{Part 3}

At context length 128, BF16 autocast gave a forward-pass peak of 2425.1 MiB and
a training-step peak of 6690.8 MiB, compared with 1656.4 MiB and 6701.1 MiB in
FP32. Thus mixed precision did not significantly reduce total memory in this
benchmark: the training-step peak was essentially unchanged because parameters,
gradients, and Adam state dominate, and the forward pass actually used more
memory due to autocast overhead/cached converted tensors on this run.

\subsection*{Part 4}

For the large model at the reference hyperparameters, a residual-stream activation
tensor has shape $4 \times 128 \times 1024$. In single precision this is
$4 \cdot 128 \cdot 1024 \cdot 4 = 2{,}097{,}152$ bytes, or exactly $2.0$ MiB
after dividing by $1024^2$.
