\section{Problem 2.4 -- Nsight Profiling}

\subsection*{Part 1}

For the small model at context length 128, the measured forward-pass NVTX range took 26.3 ms in Nsight Systems, which is in the same order as the Python timing but lower because this profile measured one steady-state step after warmup rather than averaging a longer run. The forward pass was dominated by the cuBLAS GEMM kernel \texttt{ampere\_sgemm\_128x64\_tn}, which took 81.818 ms cumulatively across the profile and was invoked 342 times total, or about 57 times per forward pass over the 6 profiled forward passes.

Besides matrix multiplication, nontrivial CUDA runtime came from elementwise multiply/add/copy kernels, sigmoid, attention masking via \texttt{where}, and softmax-related max, exp, divide, and sum-reduction kernels. When profiling both forward and backward, the same \texttt{ampere\_sgemm\_128x64\_tn} kernel remained the largest single cumulative kernel at 76.000 ms, but backward also introduced large GEMM kernels such as \texttt{ampere\_sgemm\_128x64\_nn} at 63.176 ms and \texttt{ampere\_sgemm\_128x64\_nt} at 58.041 ms, along with more elementwise and reduction work.

For a full AdamW training step, the measured NVTX range was 134.6 ms, with 44.9 ms in the measured forward pass, 50.3 ms in backward, and 46.6 ms in the optimizer step. Compared with inference, the fraction of time spent outside matrix multiplication increased because backward and AdamW add many elementwise update, copy, memset, and reduction kernels in addition to the GEMMs.

Within self-attention, the annotated ranges showed that softmax took 22.593 ms total, attention-score matmul took 12.558 ms, and the final attention matmul took 5.865 ms across the profile. Thus softmax took more runtime than the final attention matmul despite having far fewer FLOPs, which suggests it is more memory-bandwidth and kernel-launch limited than the highly optimized cuBLAS matrix multiplications.
